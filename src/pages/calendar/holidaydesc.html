PASSOVER (Pesah) — In memory of the deliverance of the Children of Israel from slavery in Egypt. (Dt 16:1-8). Passover is commemorated on the l4th of Nisan (Ex. 12:2, 13:4), and is combined with the Feast of Unleavened Bread on 15 Nisan. During the seven days of Nisan 15-21, nothing with leaven is eaten. Whenever it was impossible to keep Passover at the appointed time on 14 Nisan, it was observed a month later as Second Passover, on 14 Iyyar. For Messianic believers, Passover is sacred because of the crucifixion and atoning death of Elohim’s Messiah on 14 Nisan, when on the cross He expiated the sin of our father Adam, and restored us to Elohim’s favour, and likewise atoned for our own sins and guilt. Before His trial and crucifixion, in the same night in which Iscariot betrayed Him, He instituted the ordained apostolic ministry together with the New Covenant Oblation which is the holy sacrament of the Lord’s Supper, offered in memory of Him until His second coming at the end of this age. The account is related in the books of the Gospel (Lk 22, Mt 26, 1C 11:23-29, Jn 13). The time for commemorating the Lord’s Supper is after sunset of Nisan 13, when Nisan 14 commences. Since Biblical times the day has always been reckoned from sunset to sunset, and not from midnight as in the manner of the Romans, nor from sunrise in the manner of the Egyptians.

On the first day remove the yeast from your houses, for whoever eats anything with yeast in it from the first day through the seventh must be cut off from Israel. On the first day hold a sacred assembly, and another one on the seventh day. Do no work at all on these days, except to prepare food for everyone to eat; that is all you may do.

FEAST OF WEEKS (Shavu‘oth) — PENTECOST — The Festival of the First Fruits, Lv 23:16-21, Dt 16:9-10. It is customary to adorn the synagogues with green plants and flowers. In the synagogue service, the Book of Ruth is chanted with the reading of the Ten Commandments and the account of Mount Sinai. For Messianics, the Feast of Weeks has special significance because it was on this festival (in Greek-‘pentekoste hemera’ means fiftieth day), that the Holy Spirit of the Eternal Father, was sent by the risen and ascended Saviour, and descended upon the apostles assembled with the Lord’s Mother and other disciples. And there appeared unto them cloven tongues like of fire, and it sat upon each of them. And they were all filled with the Holy Ghost, and began to speak with other tongues, as the Spirit gave them utterance. Ac 2:1-41.

DAY OF ATONEMENT (Yom Kippur) — In the Bible this is a day which Elohim commanded the Hebrew People to observe with a total fast, during which day the High Priest offered prescribed sacrifices for the expiation of sin and guilt (Lv16:29, 23:27; Nb 29:7-11). Since there has been no Temple following its destruction, in the summer of 70 A.D., it remains impossible for Jews to obey this commandment unless the Mosaic Tabernacle is found, or the Third Temple is built, and the priesthood is restored. This day of Atonement on Tishri 10 remains for Jews the most sacred occasion in the entire year, on which they attend the services in synagogues. All adults fast for 24 hours without any food or drink, unless sick or on medication. Boys under 13 and girls under 12 are exempt.

TABERNACLES (Sukkoth) The Festival of Ingathering — Farmers assembled in the autumn, to thank Elohim, after gathering in all their grapes, olives and produce of their fields. This seven-day solemnity commemorates the sojourning of the Israelites during forty years in the wilderness, dwelling in booths or huts, called “sukkoth” in Hebrew. The fragile nature of such temporary dwellings made the Israelites perceive their utter dependence on Elohim. The eighth day on Tishri 22, is a separate celebration and one of Solemn Assembly, called Shemini ‘Asereth (Ex 23:16, Lv 23:41-43, Dt 16:13-15). Tabernacles begins on the 15th day of Tishri. During the festival of Tabernacles in Israel to-day, it is customary for every household to erect a booth, “sukkah”, having at least three walls, with the roof made of leaves and branches, and in which both the family and guests take their meals. There are also those who sleep in the booth.

LOTS (Purim) — This feast was instituted under Persian rule of Xerxes I (Ahasuerus) 486-465 B.C., in the days of Mordecai and Queen Esther, to celebrate the deliverance of the Jews from Haman’s plot to kill them. Esther chapter 9. The Book of Esther is chanted in synagogues on Purim. A special meal is eaten in the afternoon, traditionally including beans and peas as a reminder of the pulse Daniel and his three companions ate in the King’s palace. In Israel on this day Jewish children dress in various costumes as Queen Esther, Haman, etc. Purim is celebrated on Adar 14 except in walled cities, such as Jerusalem, where it is celebrated on 15 Adar. This is because the Jews in the walled city of Shushan, in Elam, celebrated their deliverance on 15 Adar. In leap years, when there are thirteen months with a second month of Adar, Purim is then celebrated in the month of Second Adar.

DEDICATION (Hanukkah) The Feast of Lights — According to tradition, this festival was begun in the days of the Hasmonaean Priest Judah Maccabee and his followers in 164 B.C. after the successful rebellion against Antiochus Epiphanes of the Seleucid Grecian Empire. At dusk during the eight days of this festival, one additional light is lit for each day, until on the eighth day eight lamps are burning. This is said to commemorate the miracle of the oil which took place after the Maccabees recovered the Temple from the Greeks. It was discovered that the Greeks had defiled all the oil reserved for the seven lamps of the Temple candelabrum, called Menorah, except for one cruse, which contained only sufficient oil for the candelabrum to remain kindled for one day. However, a miracle occurred, and the oil lasted eight days, until new supplies could be obtained. In remembrance of Elohim’s miraculous intervention, the festival of Hanukkah was instituted. It begins on the 25th of Kislew (Chisleu). The first lamp, or candle, is lit on the eve, at dusk on 24 Kislew.
